Help & Documentation

Quick Start

Drop a mesh (STL or OBJ), pick a material from the NIST XCOM database, set your scanner geometry and beam energy. Then click Optimize.

The search runs a coarse 15° grid, then refines the top candidates at 1° (Ito 2020 §2.4). It returns the optimal tilt (θ) and rotation (φ), a kV recommendation, and a Tuy completeness score.

Hover any metric or control for a deeper explanation.

Reading the Results

θ tilts the part toward the detector and φ rotates it around the beam axis. Together they minimize the longest rays through the part — those are your optimal angles.

kV recommendation — the app calculates this from the peak path, your material’s XCOM attenuation, and the polyenergetic spectrum. The margin line in the Energy card accounts for spectrum model uncertainty. Add 10–30 kV of headroom if your tube allows.

Tuy completeness — the fraction of mesh faces that have at least one tangent projection angle. Below 90%, expect cone-beam artifacts during reconstruction. Extending the search range can help if the optimum is near a boundary.

Quality vs Energy — Quality prioritizes penetration uniformity for less scatter. Energy cuts kV instead. Toggle the presets to see which ranking works better for your scan — no re-optimization needed.

IntelliScan — finds the minimum projection angles needed to cover every surface. Fewer angles means shorter scan times (Butzhammer 2026).

When to Worry

Tuy below 90% — expect cone-beam artifacts. Extend the search range or adjust the orientation.

Non-watertight mesh — rays escape through open edges, so penetration reads lower than reality. Fix the mesh gap in your CAD tool.

Beam-hardening — not modeled in the optimizer yet. Use the pre-filter panel to estimate effective energy (Eeff) manually.

Scatter is not modeled in the optimizer. The objective is purely geometric — real scans will have additional noise.
